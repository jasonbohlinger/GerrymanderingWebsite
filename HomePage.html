What is Gerrymandering?

Gerrymandering is the intentional manipulation of vote district boundaries to favor one group/party over another. We see this in the United States from both parties, who look to either maintain their control through controlling who votes for them or by trying to minimize the value of their opponents' votes and power. This is often done on the state level by the state legislature, who draw the districts for their state that are used to vote in national elections, for representation in the house of representatives. The effects of gerrymandering can be seen not only in national elections, but also in much smaller scales like school districts. School districts are gerrymandered to keep low income students out, which is often segregating people by race. Because our voting system has winner-take-all electoral systems in districts, a legislative body is elected by dividing the jurisdiction into geographic constituencies, each electing exactly one representative. Although this may result in diverse representation, due to different political groups making up majorities of different districts, within each district, only one political party or group will be able to select a candidate.

Computer programming, otherwise known as coding, is the process of performing a particular computation, usually by designing/building an executable computer program. Programming involves tasks such as analysis, generating algorithms, profiling algorithms' accuracy and resource consumption, and the implementation of algorithms.

How Can Coding Combine With Gerrymandering?

For years, the politics of redistricting have led individuals to commit this dishonest act. Although the definition of gerrymandering is clear, it is sometimes difficult to point out and find examples and proof of gerrymandering. The use of programming and algorithms has been highlighted as a theoretical solution to unbiasedly drawing legislative boundaries. People have been trying to accomplish this task by identifying gerrymandering, quantifying gerrymandering, and finally, redrawing district boundaries to fix the gerrymandering.

Why is Our Project Important?

We have discovered that many people (especially fellow students at Chapman) do not truly understand what Gerrymandering is. We want to raise awareness and educate people about gerrymandering, and the effects it has on our democracy.
